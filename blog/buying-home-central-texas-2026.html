Buying a Home in Central Texas in 2026: What You Need to Know

📅 April 2026  ·  CentralTexas.online Editorial

Central Texas's housing market has been one of the most dynamic in the country for the past five years, and 2026 brings its own set of conditions that buyers need to understand before making one of the largest financial decisions of their lives. Here's the honest guide.

Current Market Conditions (2026)

Central Texas's housing market has moderated significantly from the extreme seller's market of 2021-2022, when homes sold over asking within days and cash offers were required to compete. The 2024-2025 correction, combined with higher mortgage rates, has produced a market that is more balanced — homes spending longer on the market, price reductions becoming more common, and buyers having more negotiating leverage than they've had since 2019. This is not a buyer's market, but it's also not the panic-buying environment that defined the peak.

Median home prices in Williamson County have settled at levels significantly above pre-pandemic pricing but below the 2022 peak. Specific prices vary dramatically by city and neighborhood — Round Rock's established neighborhoods command premiums that reflect school quality and infrastructure maturity. Georgetown's rapid growth has created both value opportunities and quality variation. Cedar Park remains one of the most in-demand markets due to LISD school quality.

Choosing Your City

Round Rock: Established infrastructure, excellent RRISD schools, Dell Technologies proximity, and the widest range of housing types and price points. More traffic on IH-35 corridor than Cedar Park. Best for buyers who value established community and school consistency.

Cedar Park: Leander ISD schools (consistently strong), 183A toll access to Austin, slightly newer housing stock. Premium pricing for the school quality. Best for families prioritizing schools and willing to pay for them.

Georgetown: Fastest growing in Williamson County, diverse housing stock (Sun City through new construction), the historic downtown that no other suburb can match. Infrastructure lagging population growth in some areas. Best for buyers who value community character alongside growth upside.

Pflugerville: Northeast Austin access, Round Rock ISD serves much of the city, generally more affordable than Round Rock or Cedar Park. Less developed amenity infrastructure but growing rapidly. Best for buyers prioritizing value and northeast Austin proximity.

The School District Question

For families with children, school district quality is often the primary driver of neighborhood selection — and it should be. Our Williamson County schools guide covers the major districts in detail. Critical points: verify the specific campus (not just the district) for any address, understand boundary change risks in fast-growing districts, and visit the actual campus before purchasing rather than relying on district-level ratings alone.

Working with a Local Agent

In Central Texas's complex market — new construction subdivisions, rapid boundary changes, neighborhood quality variation within zip codes — a local agent with genuine market knowledge earns their commission. The large national brokerages have local agents; the key is finding one who works specifically in your target area rather than covering the entire metro.

💡 Home Buying Tips for Central Texas
  • Home inspection matters more here: Central Texas's clay soil produces foundation movement that is normal at low levels but concerning at higher levels. Get a foundation specialist opinion if the inspector flags any movement.
  • Hail history check: Ask for the insurance claims history on any home you're serious about. Williamson County's severe hail history means some homes have had multiple claims that affect insurability and future premiums.
  • New construction reality: Central Texas's builder activity is high but builder capacity is stretched. New construction timelines routinely extend 3-6 months beyond initial estimates. Build float time into your plans.
  • Flood zone awareness: Central Texas has significant flash flood risk. Pull the FEMA flood map for any property near creeks or in low-lying areas. Flood zone designation dramatically affects insurance costs and resale value.
